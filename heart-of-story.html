Heart of Story

A Kennedy Tragedy

​Lobotomies and neglectful treatment did not discriminate; the disabling operations and detrimental institutionalization cut across social classes.​
This held true for Rosemary Kennedy. Born with developmental disabilities, she was an active participant in life until her father ordered her lobotomized at 23 in 1941, after doctors told him that the procedure would calm her mood swings. It was thought that her disability brought shame to the Kennedy family.
“I don’t know how other than just from sheer devastation he [Joe Sr.] could allow her to disappear so much from his life. He had to be destroyed by it. That’s the only way I can explain it. It’s an incredibly heartbreaking story of a dad trying to help his daughter and hurting her. What could be worse?”
- Tim Shriver, Director of Special Olympics Global, Son of Eunice Kennedy Shriver
"Joe Sr. expected [Rosemary] to keep out of the newspaper columns."
- Amanda Smith, Joe Sr.'s granddaughter (Larson 155)
(The Original St. Coletta Building)
The outcome of the lobotomy could not have been more different. Rosemary, a young woman formerly full of life, spent her remaining years at the St. Coletta of Wisconsin under constant care, having lost all executive functioning.
"One of Ms. [Rosemary] Kennedy's nurses, who observed the procedure, found it so horrific that she left the medical profession entirely."
- James P. Caruso and Jason P. Sheehan, "Psychosurgery, Ethics, and Media: A History of Walter Freeman and the Lobotomy" (Caruso and Sheehan 5)

Eunice Kennedy & Camp Shriver

(Eunice Kennedy Shriver)
​Eunice, who had always been close with her sister, was determined to create more opportunities for intellectually disabled children, like those that Rosemary had been deprived of. She brought her fierce determination and inspirational nature to the project, which allowed it to grow. 
"She is so full of nervous energy that even in repose she seems to give off an audible whir, like an overheating computer or household appliance ... She channeled almost all of that energy—sometimes ruthlessly—into good works." 
- Scott Stosel, Eunice Kennedy Shriver's husband's biographer (Stossel)
"Rosemary was the catalyst behind Shriver’s desire to work with mentally challenged people, especially kids."
- Jane Rogers, Smithsonian curator of "Special Olympics at 50" exhibit, in a personal interview
The destruction of her sister's life, along with the broader efforts to keep intellectually disabled people from participating in society, was Eunice’s motivation for the project.

Her project in 1962 was Camp Shriver, a summer camp where intellectually disabled children could romp around her Maryland farm, enjoying summer like other children. At Camp Shriver, counselors and members of the Kennedy family connected with the campers and recognized their humanity that had been denied for so long.

Not Without Opposition

Nevertheless, some opposed Shriver’s program, believing it would fail. In their eyes, the intellectually disabled were incapable of successfully participating in normative physical and social activities. 
“There were many undercurrents of ‘these kind of people’ cannot function in this capacity – as functioning, performing athletes. I think the intellectually disabled were pigeonholed in that mental institution mindset for so long that it took parents and advocates like Mrs. Kennedy, to change the public mindset.”
– Jane Rogers, in a personal interview 

The First Special Olympics

Over time, Camp Shriver grew into the modern Special Olympics, and in the summer of 1968, the triumphant, first-ever International Special Olympics was held.​
