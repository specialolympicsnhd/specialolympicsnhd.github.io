Long-Term Consequences

Global Reach

(Michelle Obama opens Special Olympics in Los Angeles)
(Unified Basketball Game)
(Special Olympian Holding Silver Medal)
Over fifty years, Special Olympics has grown dramatically in scope and in its impact on public discourse and policy. A triumph, it has expanded to 172 countries and is now the largest sports organization in the world for those with intellectual disabilities. ​
"Special Olympics' reach has expanded to more than 5 million athletes and Unified partners -- a new global record. Athletes in nearly 170 countries took part in more than 108,000 games and competitions."
- Special Olympics Organization
Special Olympics Impact (Our Reach)
As for practical impact, employment rates for Special Olympics participants far exceed those of nonparticipating individuals. ​
"The unemployment rate of those with intellectual disabilities in the country is 82%. If they are in Special Olympics, it is [decreased to] 50%."
- Dave Lenox, in a personal interview  
New programs are continually developed within the organization. The 1997 Healthy Athletes initiative has provided international participants with over 1.7 million free health examinations. The global Unified Partners program fosters friendships between Special Olympics athletes and typically-developing peers.
(Special Olympics Mexico swimmer Michelle has her vision tested)
(Launch of Unified Partners)

Legislative Legacy

Special Olympics has brought public attention to the needs of those with intellectual disabilities and has directly influenced relevant laws, such as the 1990 Americans With Disabilities Act (ADA).
“Many of our fellow citizens with disabilities are unemployed … They want to work, and they can work.”
- President George H.W. Bush

The ADA’s passage drove improvements in educational and public services accessibility, employment accommodations, and job security. However, ability-based discrimination remains, and debate continues regarding the role of government versus the private sector in ensuring progress for those with intellectual disabilities. ​
"The Special Olympics is not a federal program. It's a private organization. I love its work, and I have personally supported its mission ... But given our current  budget realities, the federal government cannot fund every worthy program, particularly ones that enjoy robust support from private donations."
​-Betsy DeVos, U.S. Secretary of Education
"Special Olympics is a nonpartisan organization that strongly supports policies, legislation and practices that guarantee the rights, full participation, and integration of people with intellectual disabilities ... Special Olympics programs ... are critically the responsibility not just of the volunteer sector but of our elected leaders."
-Tim Shriver, CEO of Special Olympics Global 

Scientific Achievement vs. Human Rights

Finally, there is a continued struggle with balancing scientific inquiry and human rights as exemplified by current debates on cloning and genetic engineering. While supporting scientific investigation and discovery is essential, it is equally important to ensure that vulnerable populations are protected, as they tragically weren’t during the Eugenics movement.
"Many are calling for fundamental changes to ensure that all research participants are protected, differences between research disciplines are recognized and handled appropriately  …  We believe that our proposed system of oversight accomplishes these goals, and will result in better protections for research participants and better research."
- Harold T. Shapiro, Chair of the National Bioethics Advisory Commission
